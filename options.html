Options
Basics

Options give you the right to buy or sell a stock at a set price — without being forced to. More control, less capital required.

Call = Right to Buy
Put = Right to Sell
Premium = What You Pay
Expiration = Deadline
What Are Options? — Two Types
Call Option
Right to Buy
You pay a small fee (premium) for the right to buy a stock at a locked-in price, before a set deadline. If the stock goes up — you profit.
Analogy
You put a $500 deposit on a house listed at $300K. If the house rises to $350K, you still buy at $300K. Your deposit secured a profit.
Put Option
Right to Sell
You pay a premium for the right to sell a stock at a set price. If the stock drops — your put goes up in value. It's essentially insurance.
Analogy
Car insurance. You pay a monthly fee. If an accident happens, you're covered. Puts protect your investment against stock price drops.
The Greeks — Tap Each to Expand
Δ
Delta
How much your option moves when the stock moves $1
Delta of 0.50 means if the stock rises $1, your option gains ~$0.50. Higher delta = more tied to the stock price.
Plain English: Delta = your option's "sensitivity" to stock movement.

Γ
Gamma
How quickly Delta itself is changing
Gamma accelerates your gains (and losses) as the stock moves. Highest near expiration.
Plain English: Gamma = the "speed" of Delta. High gamma = fast-shifting sensitivity.
Tap to expand ↓
Θ
Theta
How much value you lose per day from time passing
Every day that passes, your option loses value — even if the stock doesn't move. The closer to expiration, the faster the decay.
Plain English: Theta = the daily "rental cost" of holding an option. Time is your enemy.
Tap to expand ↓
V
Vega
How much expected market turbulence affects your option
When markets expect big swings, option prices go up. Vega measures this sensitivity to expected movement.
Plain English: Vega = how much "nervousness in the market" raises your option's price.

Implied Volatility (IV)
IV is the market's forecast for how wildly a stock might swing in the future. Think of it like a weather confidence rating — high IV means "expect a storm." When IV is high, options cost more.
Low IV
Calm
Market is quiet. Options are cheaper. Usually a better time to buy options.
High IV
Volatile
Market is nervous. Options are expensive. Usually a better time to sell options.
Strike Prices
The strike price is the price you agree to buy or sell the stock at. It's locked in when you purchase the option.
  • 1Closer to current price = safer, but costs more premium
  • 2Further away = cheaper premium, but needs bigger move to profit
  • 3For beginners: start at-the-money (ATM) or in-the-money (ITM)
In, At & Out of the Money
Describes where the stock price sits relative to your strike. Determines whether your option has value right now.
ITM Has value now
ATM At the line
OTM Not yet
Stock must move past the strike for OTM to become profitable
ITM Call: Strike below current price. Already profitable to exercise.
ATM: Strike = stock price. Balanced — most sensitive to movement.
OTM Call: Strike above current price. Needs the stock to rise to gain value.
Pro Tips
✓
Always give yourself plenty of time — at least 30–60 days minimum
✓
Your max loss is only what you paid (the premium) — know it before buying
✓
Start with calls on stocks you already understand well
✓
Paper trade first — practice on paper before using real money
✓
Buy options when IV is low — they're cheaper and better value
Common Mistakes
✕
Buying weekly options expiring in days — Theta destroys them fast
✕
Ignoring Theta — your option loses value every single day
✕
Buying during high IV events like earnings — you're overpaying
✕
Risking more than you can lose on one position
✕
Confusing the premium cost with the stock's total value
Key Takeaways
Calls = right to buy. Puts = right to sell. Premium = your max loss.
Delta tells you sensitivity. Theta tells you the daily cost of waiting.
Buy options when IV is low. Sell when IV is high.
Strike price is your locked-in price. Closer = safer but pricier.
ITM has value now. OTM needs the stock to move to catch up.
