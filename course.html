Chapter 1 · Stock Market Foundations

What is a Stock?

Understanding ownership, shares, and how the stock market creates wealth.

Owning a Piece of a Business

When a company wants to raise money — hire talent, build products, expand globally — one powerful method is selling pieces of itself to the public. Those pieces are called stocks (also called shares or equities).

Apple Inc. (AAPL) is a perfect example. Apple sells shares on the NASDAQ, and today millions of people own a piece of one of the world's most valuable companies.

🍕

Imagine Apple's business is divided into 15.5 billion tiny slices. Each slice is one share. If you buy 10 shares at $175 each, you've invested $1,750 and own a very small fraction of Apple. If Apple grows and those shares rise to $220 — your investment is now worth $2,200. That $450 difference is your profit.

STOCK (Share / Equity)

A unit of ownership in a publicly traded company. Owning stock means you own a fraction of that company's value, assets, and future earnings.

How the Stock Market Works

The stock market is simply a regulated digital marketplace where buyers and sellers trade shares of companies. The two biggest exchanges in the U.S. are:

🏛 NYSE — New York Stock Exchange

The world's largest stock exchange by market cap. Home to blue-chip companies like Coca-Cola, JPMorgan, and Disney. Founded in 1792.

💻 NASDAQ

The tech-forward exchange. Home to Apple (AAPL), Microsoft (MSFT), Amazon (AMZN), Google (GOOGL), and Tesla (TSLA).

Two Ways to Make Money

📈 Capital Gains

You buy 10 shares of AAPL at $170 and sell at $185. Your profit: $150 ($15 gain × 10 shares). This is capital appreciation.

💰 Dividends

Apple pays a small quarterly dividend. If you own shares, you receive cash payments just for holding the stock — passive income from ownership.

Chapter 2 · Broker Setup

Your First Brokerage Account

Your broker is the gateway to the stock market. Choosing the right one — and the right account type — sets the foundation for everything else.

What is a Broker?

A broker is the platform — app or website — that connects you to the stock market. You deposit cash, the broker holds it, and you use their interface to buy and sell stocks. Think of it as a bank account specifically built for trading.

🏛

A broker is like a real estate agent for stocks. You can't walk onto the NYSE trading floor yourself — you need a licensed intermediary. Your broker is that intermediary, and today they're entirely digital, commission-free, and open 24/7 for account management.

Account Types — Which One to Open

✅ Cash Account — Best for Beginners

You can only trade with money you've actually deposited. No borrowing. If you have $1,000 in your account, you can only buy $1,000 worth of stock. This keeps your risk completely controlled.

💳

A cash account is like a debit card — you spend only what you have. No surprises, no debt. This is where every beginner should start.

📊 Margin Account

Lets you borrow money from the broker to buy more stock. Amplifies gains — but also amplifies losses. One bad trade can exceed your account balance. Not recommended for beginners.

🏦 Roth IRA — Long-Term Growth Account

A tax-advantaged retirement account. You contribute after-tax dollars and all growth is completely tax-free. The perfect destination for profits from your trading — part of the Wealth Cycle strategy.

Key Takeaways — Broker Setup

  • Webull is the recommended platform for learning to trade with real charts
  • A Roth IRA is where your trading profits eventually go to grow tax-free
  • All major brokers are commission-free — you keep your full profit

Chapter 2A · Trading Rules

The Rules Every Beginner Must Know

Know the rules before you trade. These regulations protect you — and violating them can freeze your account.

The PDT Rule — Pattern Day Trader

What is the PDT Rule?

If you make 4 or more day trades within 5 business days in a margin account, FINRA classifies you as a "Pattern Day Trader" (PDT). This requires you to maintain at least $25,000 in your account at all times.

⚠️

Violating the PDT rule in a margin account with under $25K will restrict your account to closing positions only for 90 days. This is one of the most common mistakes new traders make.

🚦

Think of it as a speed limit for trading. With under $25,000 in a margin account, you get 3 day trades per rolling 5-day period. Cross that limit and the regulator flags your account.

✅ The Simple Solution: Cash Account

The PDT rule does not apply to cash accounts. You can make as many trades as you want — as long as your settled cash is available. This is the #1 reason beginners should use cash accounts.

Good Faith Violations (GFV)

A Good Faith Violation occurs in a cash account when you use funds from a sale before they have fully settled, then sell the newly purchased position before those funds settle.

📋 GFV Example — What NOT to Do
1
Monday: You sell AAPL shares. The proceeds are pending settlement (not yet cleared).
2
Monday (same day): You use those unsettled proceeds to buy MSFT shares immediately.
3
Tuesday: You sell the MSFT shares before the original AAPL sale settled (Wed).
4
❌ This triggers a GFV. 3 GFVs in 12 months = account restricted to settled cash only for 90 days.

⏱ Cash Settlement Period — T+2

When you sell a stock, the cash takes 2 business days to settle in your account (called T+2). Don't use proceeds from a sale for new trades until the settlement completes. Check your broker app — it will show "settled cash" vs "unsettled cash."

✅ Safe Approach

Sell AAPL on Monday → wait until Wednesday → then buy MSFT with settled funds. Clean. No violations.

❌ Violation Risk

Sell AAPL on Monday → immediately buy MSFT → sell MSFT same day or Tuesday. This is a Good Faith Violation.

Key Takeaways — Trading Rules
  • PDT rule limits day trades to 3/week in margin accounts under $25K
  • Cash accounts avoid the PDT rule entirely — use one as a beginner
  • Cash settles in T+2 (2 business days) — don't use unsettled cash
  • 3 Good Faith Violations in 12 months restricts your account for 90 days

Chapter 2B · Executing Trades

Order Types & Placing Your First Trade

How you enter and exit a trade determines your price, protection, and profitability. Master order types before you trade.

Order Types Explained

When you want to buy or sell a stock, you choose an order type. This is your instruction to the broker for exactly how to execute your trade.

Order TypeWhat It DoesBest For
Market Order Buys or sells immediately at the current best available price. Speed is guaranteed — exact price is not. When speed matters more than precision
Limit Order Sets your exact buy or sell price. Only executes at your price or better. Will not execute if price isn't reached. Best for most beginner trades — you control price
Stop Loss Automatically sells your position if price drops to your set level. Exits bad trades for you without emotion. Risk management — protecting open positions
Trailing Stop Like a stop loss, but moves up automatically as price rises. Locks in gains while letting winners continue. Protecting profits in winning trades
👟

Imagine you want to buy limited-edition AirPods. A market order is paying whatever the store charges right now. A limit order is telling eBay: "I'll only buy these if the price drops to $180." Your purchase only happens at your terms — and that's how experienced traders think about every entry.

Understanding P&L (Profit & Loss)

Profit Example — AAPL Long Trade

Buy 10 shares at $175.00
Sell 10 shares at $182.00
Profit: $70.00 ($7 × 10 shares)

Loss Example — With Stop Loss

Buy 10 shares at $175.00
Stop loss triggers at $172.00
Controlled loss: −$30.00 ($3 × 10 shares)

💡

The stop loss in the example above saved you from a potentially bigger loss. Without it, if the stock dropped to $165, you'd have lost $100 instead of $30. Stop losses are not optional — they are essential.

Building Your Watchlist

A watchlist is a list of stocks you're monitoring and studying — not necessarily buying yet. It's your trading radar. Before you buy any stock, it should spend time on your watchlist first.

📋 Beginner Watchlist Strategy

Start with 5–8 stocks in industries you understand. AAPL is the perfect first watchlist stock — it's liquid, predictable, and in the news daily. Study its price behavior for 2 weeks before your first trade.

AAPL MSFT AMZN GOOGL TSLA
Key Takeaways — Executing Trades
  • Always use limit orders for price control — never buy blind with market orders
  • Set a stop loss on every trade to protect against big unexpected losses
  • P&L = (sell price − buy price) × shares owned
  • Build a watchlist before trading — observe first, trade second

Chapter 2C · Reading the Market

Charts, Candles & Market Indicators

This is where trading becomes visual. Learn to read the language of the market through candlestick charts, volume, VWAP, and moving averages.

🐂

Bullish

Expecting prices to rise. When a stock or market trends upward, it's "bullish." Bulls charge forward with horns up — your portfolio goes up with the market.

🐻

Bearish

Expecting prices to fall. When a stock trends downward, it's "bearish." Bears swipe downward — your portfolio moves against you if you're long.

Reading Candlestick Charts

Candlesticks are the standard way to visualize price data. Each candle represents a specific time period (1 min, 5 min, 1 day, etc.) and shows four critical price points.

VWAP — Volume Weighted Average Price

VWAP is the average price of a stock weighted by volume throughout the trading day. It resets every morning at market open. Traders use it as the single most important daily reference line.

Moving Averages (9 MA & 20 MA)

Moving averages smooth out price noise to reveal the underlying trend. The 9-period MA (gold) is fast and reactive. The 20-period MA (blue) is slower and shows the bigger trend. When they cross, it signals a trend change.

Volume — Why It Matters

What is Volume?

Volume = the number of shares traded in a given period. High volume on an up move = real conviction. High volume on a down move = real selling pressure. Low volume moves are often "fakeouts."

🎵

Volume is like the crowd at a concert. A packed stadium proves the artist is popular — the energy is real. An empty room means low interest. When AAPL moves on high volume, the move means something.

Key Takeaways — Reading the Market
  • Green candles = close above open (price rose). Red = close below open (fell).
  • Price above VWAP = bullish bias. Price below VWAP = bearish bias. VWAP resets daily.
  • Golden Cross (9 MA above 20 MA) = bullish momentum. Death Cross = bearish.
  • Volume confirms moves — high volume = high conviction in either direction.

Chapter 4B · Technical Analysis

Chart Patterns & Technical Setups

Support, resistance, breakouts, and key patterns — the visual language of profitable trading.

Support & Resistance — The Foundation

These are the two most important levels in all of technical analysis. Every professional trader watches them.

🛡 Support — The Floor

A price level where buyers consistently step in to stop the decline. The stock "bounces" off this level repeatedly. Think of it as the floor of a room — price tends to stop falling and reverse here.

🔴 Resistance — The Ceiling

A price level where sellers consistently step in to stop the advance. The stock "rejects" this level repeatedly. Think of it as the ceiling — price tends to stop rising and pull back here.

Support & Resistance — AAPL
AAPL · Daily Chart
AAPL bounces off support (~$172) twice and gets rejected at resistance (~$182) twice. This is a classic trading range. Buy near support, sell near resistance.
🎯

Imagine a rubber ball bouncing inside a box. The floor is support (price bounces back up), and the ceiling is resistance (price bounces back down). Key insight: When price finally breaks through the ceiling with high volume, that ceiling becomes the new floor!

Double Bottom — Bullish Reversal Pattern

The double bottom (shaped like a "W") signals that sellers have tried twice to push price lower and failed both times. It's one of the most reliable reversal patterns for new traders.

Double Bottom Pattern — AAPL
AAPL · Swing Trade Setup
AAPL forms a double bottom at ~$170 (two equal lows). When price breaks above the neckline (~$178) with momentum, the pattern confirms. Entry: on the breakout above the neckline. Target: the height of the "W" projected upward.
Breakout — The Most Powerful Setup

A breakout occurs when price decisively moves above a resistance level on high volume. The resistance that once capped price now becomes the new support floor.

Breakout Above Resistance — AAPL
AAPL · Breakout Setup
AAPL consolidates tightly below resistance at $181 for 10 sessions. Then a high-volume breakout candle clears the level. Rule: Volume must confirm the breakout — if volume is low, it's likely a fakeout.
Common Chart Patterns — Quick Reference

📐 Bull Flag

Strong upward move (the pole), then a brief consolidation pullback (the flag). A bullish continuation — price tends to break higher from the flag.

ContinuationBullish

📉 Double Bottom

Price hits a low, bounces, revisits the same low, then bounces stronger. "W" shape. Bullish reversal — enter on the breakout above the neckline.

ReversalHigh Probability

☕ Cup & Handle

Rounded bottom (cup) followed by a slight dip (handle), then breakout. Longer pattern — often 2-6 weeks. Strong bullish continuation for investors.

Longer TimeframeBullish
Key Takeaways — Technical Analysis
  • Support = price floor where buyers step in. Resistance = ceiling where sellers step in.
  • When resistance breaks on high volume, it often becomes new support — a key rule.
  • Double Bottom (W shape) = failed bearish breakdown. Enter on neckline breakout.
  • Breakouts need high volume confirmation — low volume breakouts often fail.
  • Use 2 indicators maximum as a beginner: 9/20 MA + VWAP. Simplicity wins.

Course 2 — Options Trading

Why Trade Options?

Options give you the ability to control 100 shares of AAPL for a fraction of the cost — with defined, limited risk. Here's why serious traders use them.

Welcome to Course 2

What is an Options Contract?

An option is a contract that gives you the right — but not the obligation — to buy or sell 100 shares of a stock at a specific price, before a specific date. You pay a smaller "premium" for this right instead of buying 100 full shares outright.

OPTIONS CONTRACT

One contract = rights over 100 shares. You pay a premium (the contract price) for this right. If the trade goes your way, your percentage return can be far greater than buying the stock outright.

🏠

Imagine AAPL stock is at $175. To buy 100 shares, you'd need $17,500. Instead, you pay a $350 premium for an options contract giving you the right to buy those 100 shares at $175 for the next 30 days. If AAPL jumps to $185, your contract is worth much more — and your return on $350 is enormous compared to what you'd make on $17,500. That's leverage.

Why Options Beat Stocks in Specific Situations

💡 Leverage

Control 100 shares of AAPL (~$17,800) for as little as $300–$500. Your percentage gains on a winning trade can be 3x–10x what you'd make holding the stock.

🛡 Capped Risk (As a Buyer)

The most you can lose is the premium you paid. Buy a $350 AAPL call and that's your maximum loss — even if AAPL crashes to zero. Your downside is always defined.

📈 Profit in Any Direction

Calls profit when AAPL goes UP. Puts profit when AAPL goes DOWN. No other vehicle gives you this two-directional flexibility with defined risk.

💰 Fund Your Portfolio

Options profits fund your long-term Roth IRA stock positions. One well-timed options trade on AAPL can return what a full position in the stock takes months to accumulate.

Chapter 2 · Options

Calls & Puts — The Two Options

Every options strategy is built on just two instruments. Master these and you've unlocked the foundation of options trading.

📞

CALL Option

You believe AAPL will go UP. A call gives you the right to buy 100 shares at the strike price. If AAPL rises above your strike, your call gains value.

Bullish Strategy
🤚

PUT Option

You believe AAPL will go DOWN. A put gives you the right to sell 100 shares at the strike price. If AAPL falls below your strike, your put gains value.

Bearish Strategy
☂️

A put option is like insurance on your car. You pay a monthly premium. If something bad happens (stock drops), your insurance pays out. If nothing bad happens, you just lose the premium. Put buyers are hedging — or betting — on downside.

Understanding Strike Prices

The strike price is the price at which you can exercise your option — the locked-in buy price (call) or sell price (put). It's the price you're betting the stock will reach.

Call Example — AAPL $180 Strike

AAPL is at $178. You buy a call with a $180 strike. If AAPL rises above $180 before expiration, your call gains value. The further above $180 it goes, the more profitable your contract.

AAPL > $180 = Profit

Put Example — AAPL $175 Strike

AAPL is at $178. You buy a put with a $175 strike. If AAPL falls below $175, your put gains value. The further below $175 it falls, the more your put is worth.

AAPL < $175 = Profit

How to Choose: 3 Key Decisions

1. Direction

Bullish on AAPL? Buy a call. Bearish? Buy a put. Be crystal clear on your thesis before selecting anything else.

2. Strike Price

Start with ATM (At The Money) strikes — near AAPL's current price. More expensive than OTM but higher probability and better Delta sensitivity.

3. Expiration

Give yourself enough time. 30–60 days until expiration is the beginner sweet spot. Avoid options expiring in less than 2 weeks — time decay gets brutal.

Key Takeaways — Calls & Puts
  • Call options = bullish bet (AAPL goes UP above the strike)
  • Put options = bearish bet (AAPL goes DOWN below the strike)
  • ATM (At The Money) options are the best starting point for beginners
  • Always choose 30–60 day expirations as a beginner — time is your ally

Chapter 2A · Options Details

The Greeks & Implied Volatility

These are the forces that move option prices every single day. You don't need to be a mathematician — you need to understand what each means practically.

The Four Greeks — Simplified
Δ
Delta

How much your option price moves per $1 move in AAPL. A Delta of 0.50 means your AAPL call gains $50 for every $1 rise in AAPL (per contract = 100 shares). ATM options have Delta ~0.50. ITM = higher Delta. OTM = lower Delta.

Θ
Theta — Time Decay

How much your option loses every single day just from time passing. If AAPL does nothing, your option loses value daily. Theta decay accelerates as expiration approaches — this is why beginners must avoid very short-dated options.

Γ
Gamma

How quickly Delta itself changes as AAPL moves. High Gamma means your Delta (and P&L) changes rapidly. Important context, but not something beginners need to actively trade on.

V
Vega — Volatility

How much your option moves when Implied Volatility (IV) changes. High Vega = very sensitive to volatility. Crucial to understand around AAPL earnings reports — IV spikes, then collapses after the event.

⏳

Think of Theta like an ice cream cone on a hot day. The moment you buy it, it starts melting. Even if nothing else changes, time is working against you every day. Your AAPL option needs to move in your direction fast enough to outrun Theta decay. This is why AAPL needs to move — sitting still costs you money daily.

Implied Volatility (IV) — The Price of Fear

What is IV?

Implied Volatility is the market's forecast of how much AAPL will move. High IV = market expects a big move → options are expensive. Low IV = market expects calm → options are cheaper.

⚠️ High IV — Avoid Buying

IV always spikes before AAPL earnings. Premiums are inflated. Even if AAPL moves in your direction, the "IV crush" after earnings can make your option lose value. Don't buy options right before earnings unless you know exactly what you're doing.

✅ Low IV — Good Time to Buy

When AAPL is calm and IV is low, options premiums are cheaper. You get more leverage per dollar spent. Look for periods of low IV on AAPL when you see a technical setup forming — that's your entry window.

Expiration Dates — Choosing Wisely

⚡ Weekly (0–7 days)

Extreme Theta decay. High gamma risk. Move fast or lose everything. Not for beginners. These are for experienced traders playing specific catalysts.

✅ Monthly (30–60 days)

The beginner sweet spot. Enough time for your AAPL thesis to play out without paying excessive premium. Best balance of cost and time.

📅 LEAPS (1–2+ years)

Behave like stock ownership with leverage. Very low Theta decay. Great for investors who want AAPL exposure with defined risk and capital efficiency.

⚠️

If AAPL doesn't hit your strike before expiration, you lose 100% of your premium. A $350 AAPL call can go to $0. This is why position sizing is critical — never put more into a single options contract than you can afford to lose completely.

Key Takeaways — Greeks & IV
  • Delta tells you how much your contract moves per $1 move in AAPL (~0.50 for ATM)
  • Theta = daily time decay — works against you every day. AAPL must move in your direction.
  • Buy options when IV is relatively low — you get cheaper contracts with more upside
  • Use 30–60 day expirations — gives your thesis time to play out without crushing decay
  • Never risk more on a single contract than you can afford to lose entirely
